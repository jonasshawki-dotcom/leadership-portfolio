Learning to

Jonas Shawki – Junior, Economics – Johns Hopkins

This portfolio documents a semester of thinking carefully about what leadership actually is – not as a trait or a title, but as a practice that requires constant observation, reflection, and adjustment.

“Leadership is not a fixed trait, but a practice that requires constant observation, reflection, and adjustment.”

My name is Jonas Shawki, and I am currently a junior at Johns Hopkins University studying Economics with a strong interest in finance and leadership within high-performance environments. This semester, I am taking Leadership Theory, and this website serves as a space to reflect on what I am learning and how it is shaping the way I think about myself as both a leader and a teammate.

At the start of this semester, I viewed leadership primarily as competence and decisiveness – the ability to make strong decisions, drive results, and have people trust and follow your lead. As the course has progressed, I have begun to see leadership as more complex and relationship-driven. Leadership is not just about confidence or authority, but instead about emotional intelligence and adaptability.

This portfolio is organized into six sections. It begins with this introduction, then moves into a Reflected Best Self Portrait – a collection of stories from the people who know me best – followed by a self-reflection on my leadership journey, a reflection on my personality and strengths assessments, an example of my academic writing on leadership, and finally a section on an area of growth I am actively working on.

My hope is that this site serves not only as an academic deliverable, but as an honest record of where I am in my development – what I have learned, what I have started to question, and what I want to keep building on.

Listening Before Advising

Across all seven contributors, a consistent observation was that I tend to listen carefully and fully before responding. Rather than offering immediate solutions, I make space for people to feel genuinely heard first.

Theme 02
Mediating Conflict

Several friends noted my ability to step in when tensions arise and help de-escalate difficult conversations – not by taking sides, but by helping both parties find common ground and move forward together.

Theme 03
Reliable Presence

Multiple contributors described me as someone they turn to when things are hard – not because I always have the answer, but because I show up consistently, check in without being prompted, and remain steady under pressure.

My Best Self Portrait

I think that, at my best, I am someone who supports the people around me by ultimately being there for them. I tend to focus heavily on how people feel and what I can do to make them feel their best – particularly the friends and family I am closest to.

In life, I care the absolute most about the people around me – whether friends or family – and this translates into team settings where I prioritize people feeling heard, supported, and able to move forward together. My best self is not the person driving hardest toward a result; it is the person who makes sure no one gets left behind in the process.

The themes that emerged from these stories – listening, mediating, reliability – are not things I perform deliberately. They seem to be how I naturally show up when I am at my most present and most engaged. The portrait they paint is of someone who leads not by directing, but by supporting: creating the conditions where people feel safe enough to be honest, clear enough to move forward, and valued enough to stay.

Self-Reflection

From Control to Connection:
Reflecting on My Leadership Journey

“It is indispensable to have a habit of observation and reflection.”

– Abraham Lincoln

Early in this semester, I described leadership as the capacity to shape the behaviour of others by trust and example. I came to that definition by experience, a micromanaging boss who drained his team’s motivation, and a tennis coach who restored mine through respect and silent consistency. I came to the conclusion that authority is not a motivating factor and that leadership is all about providing the environment in which others are able to do their best.

That definition was both relational in nature and outcome-blind. I had a sense of leadership as an orientation – what you take to a room – without giving full consideration to what it yields. What this semester contributed was the other half, that is, how you lead and what it produces are inseparable. Our readings on the frameworks of leadership, especially Transformational Leadership and LMX Theory, provided me with the vocabulary to describe a phenomenon that I had been feeling but could not yet explain: that the effectiveness of leadership is not just in the way a leader acts, but in what the actions of that leader actually accomplish for the people surrounding them and the outcomes they generate.

My fundamental belief has held true; to me, leadership remains about empowering others and not dominating them. This semester, however, added nuance to that belief. Now I realize that good intentions do not always lead to good results. A leader may be truly concerned with his or her people and yet deliver unfair outcomes by being blind in the way they allocate attention, trust, and access. I also know that leadership exists at two levels at the same time: the person before you and the group to which that person is a member. It is in keeping that tension, in not taking care of one at the expense of the other, that the real challenge resides. The most significant change is that I no longer believe that leadership is something you possess or lack, but a practice. One that needs to be observed, modified, and repeated.

As Vice President of my fraternity chapter, I encountered a situation that forced me to practice what I had been studying. A subgroup of roughly eight members had gradually disengaged from the chapter. The roots of their frustration ran deeper than any single decision. Over time, through elections and natural turnover, they had lost their seats on the executive board – and with that, the decision-making influence they once had. This was a significant shift. These were members who had shaped the chapter into what it is today; they had been central to its identity. Losing that voice, and then watching judicial board decisions get made without them, created a slow accumulation of grievance that eventually expressed itself as withdrawal. They stopped showing up. They went quiet in group chats. From the outside, it looked like apathy. It was not.

I knew these members well, and I recognized what was actually happening. I reached out – individually in person, through one-on-one conversations, and in smaller group settings – without an agenda or a script. I listened. What I heard was not opposition to the chapter itself, but a feeling of being left behind by it. They still cared. They just no longer felt that the chapter reflected them or needed them.

“They still cared. They just no longer felt that the chapter reflected them or needed them.”

Once I understood that, I worked to close that gap – not by reversing decisions that had been made through legitimate processes, but by making sure their perspective was heard within the executive board and that they felt genuinely connected to what the chapter was building. The change was gradual. Attendance crept back. Participation returned. By the end of the semester, they showed up to our most significant brotherhood event of the year – our Ocean City trip – which, for a group that had been functionally absent, was not a small thing.

The Theory Behind the Experience

What I was doing, without naming it at the time, aligns closely with Leader–Member Exchange (LMX) Theory. LMX holds that leadership is not a message broadcast equally to everyone – it is a series of individual relationships, each with its own quality. High-quality exchanges are built on trust, mutual respect, and genuine investment. Low-quality ones are transactional: the follower fulfills their obligations, and little more. Critically, the theory predicts that followers in high-quality relationships perform better, feel more committed, and are more likely to contribute beyond what is formally required of them.

What had happened with this subgroup was a deterioration in exchange quality – not because anyone had acted in bad faith, but because the structural shift of losing executive positions had weakened their connection to the organization’s leadership. The relational infrastructure that once made them central had quietly eroded. My intervention was, in LMX terms, an attempt to rebuild that exchange quality – to re-establish the trust, recognition, and mutual investment that had been lost. The outcome bore this out: re-engagement was not transactional. They did not show up because they were asked to. They showed up because they felt, again, that it was theirs to show up to.

This also connects to what we studied through Transformational Leadership Theory. Transformational leaders attend to followers’ individual needs – what Bass and Avolio call individualized consideration – and work to align personal investment with collective purpose. Bringing this group back was not just about their attendance numbers. It was about restoring their sense of belonging to something they had helped build.

Drawbacks and Challenges

This approach is not without its limitations. Relational leadership is time-intensive and does not scale easily. It worked here in part because I already had trust with this subgroup – I was not building a relationship from scratch under pressure. A leader without that existing foundation would have had far less access, and the same approach might have landed very differently.

LMX theory itself surfaces a harder problem: the relational investment that makes high-quality exchanges powerful is finite. Time, attention, and emotional energy are limited resources. A leader who invests deeply in repairing one set of relationships may be neglecting others in the process. The risk is that even well-intentioned relational leadership produces its own in-groups – just different ones.

There is also a structural limitation that this experience made clear to me. What I did worked, but it was informal and ad hoc. It relied on my personal proximity to that group and on my reading of the situation. An organization that depends on individual relationships to manage discontent is fragile. The deeper fix – and one the chapter has not fully addressed – is building deliberative processes that prevent this kind of disengagement from accumulating in the first place: clearer communication around elections, structured channels for dissent, and ways for members outside the executive board to feel meaningfully connected to decisions.

Moving Forward

This experience confirmed what the semester reinforced: leadership is not a position. It is a repeated choice to remain present and attentive – especially when the people who need attention are the ones who have gone quiet. Going forward, I want to be more intentional about building high-quality relationships before they need repair, not as a response to withdrawal but as a proactive investment in the people around me. I also want to think more seriously about structure – about how the organizations I am part of can build the conditions for belonging institutionally, rather than relying on individual leaders to patch it together relationally.

My definition of leadership has not changed so much as it has deepened. Creating conditions where others can perform at their absolute best still means enabling, not controlling. But I understand now that those conditions are fragile, that they require maintenance, and that the work of building them is never finished.

Commander and Empath:
Understanding My Leadership Wiring

“My strengths in empathy, harmony, and positivity help me remain in tune with the human side of leadership.”

From my assessment reflection

16 Personalities: ENTJ-A (The Commander)

My 16 Personalities assessment designated me as an ENTJ-A, or a Commander – a personality type associated with leadership, strategic thinking, and decisiveness. People with this personality type tend to enjoy organizing people and resources toward a goal and driving progress within teams.

Reflecting on my own experiences, I can see how these characteristics resonate with how I approach leadership in certain environments. I naturally find myself wanting to organize tasks and create plans to ensure the team’s goals and deadlines are reached. At the same time, the assessment reminded me that effective leadership requires balance – focusing only on results can overlook team dynamics. This tension became visible during my experience managing the disengaged subgroup within my fraternity: my ENTJ instinct was to identify the problem and resolve it efficiently, but what the situation required was patience and listening.

CliftonStrengths: My Top Five

EmpathyRelationship Building
HarmonyRelationship Building
ConsistencyExecuting
PositivityInfluencing

Empathy, my strongest theme, reflects the ability to understand and feel the emotions of others. In leadership situations, this helps me recognize when teammates may be stressed or struggling and respond to those realities rather than ignoring them in pursuit of outcomes.

Harmony reflects a preference for minimizing unnecessary conflict and focusing on areas of agreement. I often act as a mediator when disagreements arise, identifying practical solutions that allow the team to move forward. This strength was directly activated during the fraternity situation in my self-reflection.

Consistency highlights my preference for fairness, structure, and clear processes. I try to ensure that responsibilities are distributed fairly and that everyone understands expectations – not just those closest to leadership.

Positivity reflects an optimistic and energetic approach to working with others. I often try to keep team morale high, particularly during demanding or stressful periods when momentum is most at risk.

Communication represents my ability to express ideas clearly and engage others in discussion. I am often the person who translates complex situations into language everyone can engage with.

When Both Assessments Are Read Together

The most interesting insight from holding both assessments together is the tension they reveal. The ENTJ type pushes toward direction, strategy, and achievement. My five strengths pull in a relational direction: empathy, harmony, positivity, consistency, fairness.

“The ENTJ pulls toward results. The strengths pull toward people. The best version of my leadership holds both.”

This combination suggests that my leadership approach involves not only organizing teams to maximize their abilities, but also ensuring the people within those teams feel supported. Moving forward, I hope to develop this balance further – becoming a leader who can cultivate strong relationships and drive results, understanding that the two are more connected than they appear.

Example of Writing on Leadership

What Makes a Leader Effective?
Two Frameworks, One Leader

“Leadership is complex and context-dependent; no single framework captures its entirety.”

From the essay

This piece assesses the leadership of the current president of my fraternity at Johns Hopkins University. As president, he oversees executive board operations, member accountability, philanthropy initiatives, alumni relations, and risk management. His leadership takes place in a high-pressure environment where organizational culture, academic performance, and external university oversight intersect. During this academic year, the chapter faced recruitment challenges, internal conflict among members, and increasing administrative scrutiny. These circumstances created a complex environment where leadership requires both relational sensitivity and strategic direction.

To critically evaluate his leadership effectiveness, I analyze his approach using two frameworks: Transformational Leadership Theory (Bass & Avolio, 1994) and Leader–Member Exchange Theory, or LMX (Graen & Uhl-Bien, 1995). These frameworks allow for examination of both his motivational influence on the organization as a whole and the quality of his relationships with individual members.

Transformational Leadership

Transformational leadership emphasizes a leader’s ability to inspire followers beyond self-interest. It operates through four components: idealized influence (modeling integrity), inspirational motivation (articulating a compelling vision), intellectual stimulation (encouraging creative thinking), and individualized consideration (attending to each person’s needs). Transformational leaders elevate followers’ values, foster commitment to collective goals, and encourage innovation.

Our president demonstrates strong inspirational motivation. At the beginning of the semester, recruitment numbers were declining, and morale had weakened following a disciplinary investigation. Rather than focusing on blame, he reframed the situation as an opportunity to “redefine what our chapter stands for.” He articulated a clear vision centered on accountability, brotherhood, and long-term sustainability. This reframing shifted conversations from defensiveness to forward-thinking action. As a result, executive board members became more proactive in outreach and programming.

He also exhibits idealized influence by modeling the standards he expects. During a period when academic performance across the chapter declined, he voluntarily published his own GPA and study schedule to normalize academic accountability. This behavior strengthened his credibility and encouraged transparency among members. Transformational leaders gain trust by aligning words and actions; his consistency reinforced organizational norms.

However, his leadership is less strong in intellectual stimulation. Executive meetings tend to prioritize efficiency over creativity. While decisions are clear and decisive, alternative perspectives are sometimes not fully explored. During philanthropy planning, for instance, he selected a traditional format without fully engaging the board in brainstorming new approaches. Transformational leadership encourages challenging assumptions and inviting dissent (Northouse, 2021). Increasing structured opportunities for debate could enhance innovation and shared ownership.

Overall, through the transformational lens, his leadership has strengthened morale, clarified vision, and improved accountability. Recruitment numbers rebounded strongly, and chapter GPA improved the following semester. Yet fostering more intellectual engagement could further elevate performance.

Leader–Member Exchange (LMX) Theory

While transformational leadership focuses on vision and inspiration, LMX Theory shifts attention to the individual relationships between a leader and each of their followers. High-quality exchanges are characterized by trust, respect, and mutual obligation – the follower feels genuinely invested in, and gives more in return. Low-quality exchanges are more transactional and role-bound: the follower does what is required, and little more.

Our president maintains high-quality relationships with core executive members. He schedules one-on-one check-ins monthly, asks about academic and personal stressors, and adapts expectations when necessary. These behaviors reflect strong relational investment, which have helped retain executive board members and keep the inner team cohesive.

However, LMX theory also highlights the potential formation of “in-groups” and “out-groups.” Within the broader chapter, some general members perceive that executive leaders have closer access to the president. Informal social proximity appears to influence communication frequency. While not intentional, this dynamic risks creating perceived favoritism. Research suggests that differential exchanges can impact organizational cohesion (Graen & Uhl-Bien, 1995). To strengthen his leadership, he could intentionally broaden high-quality exchanges beyond the executive board. Structured listening sessions or rotating small-group meetings could help expand relational trust across the chapter.

“A leader may excel at vision yet unintentionally allow relational gaps to form.”

What Both Frameworks Reveal Together

Evaluating this leader through both frameworks reveals an important insight: inspirational vision and relational depth do not automatically scale equally across an organization. Transformational leadership explains his ability to motivate and elevate standards, while LMX reveals uneven relational distribution within the broader membership. A leader may excel at vision yet unintentionally allow relational gaps to form.

This assessment demonstrates that the president of my fraternity is largely successful, particularly in articulating vision and modeling accountability. But the benefit of understanding multiple leadership theories lies precisely in this: the ability to evaluate leadership from different dimensions – motivation, relationships, structure, and fairness. Leadership is complex and context-dependent; no single framework captures its entirety. By applying multiple approaches, we gain a deeper, more critical understanding of both strengths and developmental opportunities.

Growth

Making Space
for Every Voice

“Effective leadership is about ensuring others feel included and empowered to contribute.”

From my introduction

The Area I Am Working On

One area of growth I am currently focused on is ensuring intentional space for everyone in group discussions. I tend to be very solutions-oriented, especially in academic or professional team settings. While this often helps maintain efficiency, I have realized that effective leadership is not just about arriving at the right answer – it is about ensuring others feel included and empowered to contribute to finding it.

This tendency connects directly to what my 16 Personalities assessment identified as a key ENTJ challenge: the drive toward decisive action can come at the cost of fully drawing out the perspectives of those around me. I can move quickly, and in doing so, I risk creating a dynamic where quieter voices feel their input is not needed – even when it would genuinely improve the outcome.

My Concrete Short-Term Action

A concrete short-term action I plan to implement is intentionally pausing before finalizing any group decisions and inviting input from each team member to ensure their point of view has been heard. I will ask open-ended questions such as “What are your thoughts on this?” or “Do you have any concerns or other ideas?” This small adjustment will encourage broader participation and build psychological safety within the team.

How This Will Strengthen My Team

Improving in this area will directly strengthen the executive board of my fraternity – the team I am currently most actively leading. By promoting deeper discussion and more collaborative decision-making, I can help ensure that the people around the table feel genuinely heard rather than simply consulted after the fact. This matters especially given what I learned through the LMX framework: when people feel their exchange with leadership is low-quality, they gradually disengage. The eight members who withdrew from chapter life this semester were, in part, a product of accumulated moments where they felt unheard.

Beyond the immediate team, this growth also connects to the Best Self Portrait in Tab 2. The themes that emerged from the people who know me best – listening before advising, making space for others, being a reliable presence – are exactly the qualities this commitment is designed to activate more consistently in formal leadership settings.
